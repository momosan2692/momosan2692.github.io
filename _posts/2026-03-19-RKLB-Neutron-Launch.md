## 3. ATM Reference Guide — What Every Investor Must Know

### 3.1 What Is an ATM Offering?

An **at-the-market (ATM) equity offering** is a mechanism that allows a publicly listed company to sell newly issued shares directly into the open market, over time, at prevailing market prices — without a traditional underwritten deal, roadshow, or fixed discount.

Unlike a follow-on offering (where a bank underwrites a block at a fixed discount on a single day), an ATM is:

| Characteristic | ATM Offering | Traditional Follow-On |
|---|---|---|
| Timing | Continuous, over weeks/months | Single day or overnight |
| Price | Market price at time of sale | Fixed, typically 5–8% discount |
| Commitment | Discretionary — company can pause | Fully committed once priced |
| Transparency | Low (shares sold quietly through agents) | High (public roadshow) |
| Market impact | Gradual, sustained pressure | Sharp one-time drop |
| Dilution | Spread over time | Immediate and known |

**The key legal documents:**

| Document | Role | Timing |
|---|---|---|
| **S-3 Shelf Registration** | Pre-approval — reserves the right to sell up to $X | Filed months/years in advance |
| **424B5 Prospectus Supplement** | Activation — specifies exact amount, agents, use of proceeds | Filed the day the ATM launches |

---

### 3.2 How to Read a 424B5 Filing

The **424B5** is the single most important document an investor can read around an ATM event. It contains the most legally binding disclosures the company will ever make — any omission of material fact constitutes securities fraud.

**The two-document system:**

**Step 1 — S-3 Shelf** (filed months/years in advance):
> *"We reserve the right to sell up to $X billion in securities at some point in the future."*

General, forward-looking, no specific terms. Sits unused until needed.

**Step 2 — 424B5 Supplement** (filed when the ATM activates):
> *"We are now actually selling. Here is the exact amount, agents, mechanism, use of proceeds, and risk factors."*

The number code decoded:

| Code element | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **424** | SEC rule family governing prospectus filings |
| **B** | Filed *after* the offering price is determined |
| **5** | A *supplement* to a previously filed base shelf prospectus |

**The mortgage analogy:**
- The **S-3 shelf** = getting pre-approved for a mortgage. The bank says "you qualify for up to $X." Nothing is borrowed yet.
- The **424B5** = actually drawing down on that pre-approval for a specific purchase at a specific price, with all terms legally binding.

**What to look for in a 424B5:**

1. **Aggregate offering amount** — the maximum ceiling, not a commitment to sell everything
2. **Sales agents** — the banks executing the sales; more prestigious agents signal confidence
3. **Use of proceeds** — vague language ("general corporate purposes") is a yellow flag; specific language is more accountable
4. **Prior program disclosure** — whether this replaces an existing ATM and how much was drawn
5. **Risk factors** — the most honest section; hardware failures and going-concern doubts appear here first
6. **Current share count** — the denominator for all dilution math; always use this, not a financial terminal
7. **Forward sale agreement structure** — advanced mechanism allowing the company to lock in floor prices for some sales

### 3.3 The Dilution Math — Correctly Calculated

**Dilution % = (ATM Amount ÷ Stock Price) ÷ Shares Outstanding**

**Always use the most current shares outstanding from the filing itself — not from a financial data terminal, which may lag by a quarter.**

**RKLB example — the corrected math:**

| Input | Wrong (stale data) | Correct (from filing) |
|---|---|---|
| ATM size | $1,000,000,000 | $1,000,000,000 |
| Stock price | $72 | $72 |
| New shares needed | 13.9M | 13.9M |
| Shares outstanding used | ~408M (2024 figure) | **~605M** (Q1 2026 guided) |
| **Dilution** | **3.4%** | **2.3%** |

The 605M figure reflects convertible preferred share conversions and new shares from convertible note conversions in early Q1 2026 — none of which appear in older databases.

### 3.4 The Reflexivity Loop — And Its Circuit Breaker

When an ATM hits, a potential feedback loop emerges:

```
ATM announced → stock drops → lower price → more shares needed
→ more dilution → stock drops further → more shares needed → ...
```

This loop is real — but it has a natural circuit breaker: **management self-interest.**

The ATM is not a commitment to sell $1B immediately. It is maximum capacity at management's discretion. If the stock falls to $48, management can and almost certainly would **pause ATM selling** — issuing 20.8M shares at $48 is far more expensive for existing shareholders than 12.5M shares at $80. Executives own significant stock personally. They feel that dilution.

**RKLB historical pattern:**

| ATM Event | Immediate drop | Subsequent move |
|---|---|---|
| Sep 2025 ATM ($750M filed) | ~8% | +22% within 60 days (Neutron progress) |
| Jan 2026 tank failure | ~12% | Partial recovery to ATH $99.58 |
| Mar 2026 ATM ($1B filed) | ~10% | TBD — contingent on tank requalification |

### 3.5 The Existing Shareholder's Edge

An investor who holds existing shares **and** has dry cash occupies a structurally advantaged position when an ATM drop occurs.

**What the ATM seller actually is:**
- Does not have a fundamental view on the company
- Does not care if it sells at $72 or $68
- Creates predictable, bounded, temporary downward pressure

This is a **price-agnostic liquidity event** — not a panic seller, not an informed seller. When a non-fundamental seller meets a fundamental buyer at the same price, the fundamental buyer wins over time — as long as the underlying thesis is correct.

**The honest risk assessment:**
The logic only works if the fundamental thesis is intact. The investor must honestly ask: *is this drop about dilution mechanics, or is the business actually impaired?*

For RKLB in March 2026: dilution mechanics + unresolved hardware risk. Both matter:
- **Dilution mechanics** alone → drop is likely an overreaction → adding is defensible
- **Unresolved hardware risk** → drop reflects genuine uncertainty → position sizing discipline is paramount

### 3.6 ATM Checklist Before You Act

**Step 1 — Find and read the 424B5**
- [ ] What is the exact ATM size?
- [ ] Is this a new program or a replacement? How much was drawn from the prior one?
- [ ] What is the stated use of proceeds?
- [ ] What risk factors are newly disclosed?
- [ ] What is the current shares outstanding per the filing?

**Step 2 — Calculate the correct dilution**
- [ ] New shares = ATM size ÷ current stock price
- [ ] Dilution % = new shares ÷ shares outstanding from filing
- [ ] Is the stock drop proportional to actual dilution, or is it an overreaction?

**Step 3 — Assess the business context**
- [ ] Why is the company raising capital? Opportunistic or necessity?
- [ ] Is there an unresolved fundamental problem disclosed in the filing?
- [ ] What is the cash runway with and without this raise?
- [ ] How many ATMs in the past 12 months? Pattern matters.
- [ ] Is the cash an enabling condition for existing valuation, or genuinely additive?

**Step 4 — Evaluate your position**
- [ ] Is the drop about ATM mechanics or real business deterioration?
- [ ] What is your conviction level on the underlying thesis?
- [ ] Do you have dry powder, and is your position sizing appropriate?
- [ ] What catalyst ends the ATM selling pressure?

**Step 5 — Decide and size**
- [ ] If adding: what is your maximum loss if the bear case materialises?
- [ ] If holding: is current position size still appropriate given updated risks?
- [ ] If exiting: are you selling because of mechanics (likely wrong) or because the thesis is broken (possibly right)?

## 5. RKLB Valuation — 估值

### 5.1 Why ATM Cash Does Not Add Net Value (On Day One)

When RKLB raises $1B via ATM, two things happen simultaneously:

| Event | Effect |
|---|---|
| Cash increases by $1B | +$1B to assets |
| 13.9M new shares issued | +$1B to equity base (at $72/share) |

They exactly cancel. The company is worth the same — it has more cash and more shares. Each share's proportional claim on the company is diluted by exactly the same amount the cash was added.

**Enterprise Value is unchanged:**

```
Enterprise Value = Market Cap + Debt − Cash

Before ATM:  $43.6B − $1.1B cash  =  EV ~$42.5B
After ATM:   $44.6B − $2.1B cash  =  EV ~$42.5B
```

**Per-share value is unchanged (on pure math):**

```
Before: $43.6B ÷ 605M shares  =  $72.07/share
After:  $44.6B ÷ 618.9M shares =  $72.07/share
```

**The total pie stays the same size. It is just cut into more slices.**

### 5.2 But the Cash Is Not Ignored Either

The ATM cash plays two distinct roles that are easily confused:

| Role | What it means |
|---|---|
| **Enabling condition** | Without this cash, Neutron may not complete → existing $88–120 valuation collapses |
| **Future value creator** | With this cash deployed into Neutron success → NSSL unlocks → re-rating to $120+ |

The cash is **not additive** to the current valuation.
It is **protective** of the current valuation.

**The correct framing:**

> Before ATM: RKLB is worth $72 *assuming* it can fund Neutron to completion.
>
> After ATM: RKLB is worth $72 *with greater certainty* that it can fund Neutron to completion.

The stock should theoretically go **up slightly** on an ATM — because funding risk just decreased. The 10% drop tells you the market weighted dilution fear and the tank failure disclosure more heavily than the reduced funding risk. That is the mispricing worth understanding.

**Where ATM cash genuinely adds new value:**

| Cash Deployment | Already in model? | Creates new value? |
|---|---|---|
| Neutron development completion | ✅ Yes — assumed | ❌ Enables, not adds |
| Acquisition of new capability | ❌ Not modelled | ✅ Genuinely additive |
| Share buyback below intrinsic value | ❌ Not modelled | ✅ Accretive |
| Sitting in money market | ❌ Not modelled | ⚠️ Marginal interest income only |

### 5.4 The Cash Flow Flip

Once Neutron achieves first orbit, the capital story inverts permanently:

```
2026 Q4        2027              2028              2029+
    │              │                 │                 │
Neutron       First NSSL        TRKT3 sats        Full rate
Flight 1  →   task orders   →   delivering    →   production
    │              │                 │                 │
Last major    Cash starts      Cash flow         ATM becomes
ATM draw      covering ops     positive?         unnecessary
```

**Why government contracts are particularly powerful here:**

Government contracts — NSSL task orders and SDA progress payments — pay **milestones throughout the build**, not just on delivery. This means:

| Contract | Cash Flow Nature |
|---|---|
| NSSL task orders ($50–150M each) | Milestone payments — cash arrives **before** launch |
| SDA TRKT3 ($816M) | Progress payments throughout 2027–2029 build |
| MACH-TB HASTE ($190M) | Per-mission payments — already flowing now |

**The backlog is not just revenue — it is pre-funded production capital.** The $1.85B effectively becomes a self-funding production line once Neutron qualifies for government work.

**Steady-state production model (2028 estimate):**

```
8 Neutron launches × $35–40M avg     =  $280–320M/yr
+ 20+ Electron launches × $8M        =  $160M/yr
+ Space systems (SDA, HASTE, etc.)   =  $600–800M/yr
────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Total revenue                        =  ~$1.1–1.3B/yr

Operating costs (estimated)          =  ~$800–900M/yr
────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Operating cash flow                  =  $200–400M/yr
```

At that point, RKLB not only stops needing ATMs — it potentially **buys back the shares it diluted.**

## 11. Conclusion

### On the ATM

The $1B ATM is not evidence of distress. It is the **necessary bridge financing** that converts Neutron from a probability into a near-certainty — protecting the $88–120 analyst targets that require Neutron to fly. The 2.3% dilution is real but the 10% drop is a 4.3:1 overreaction. The excess decline reflects pattern risk (two ATMs in six months) and the tank failure disclosure — both legitimate concerns, but neither changes the fundamental value of the business.

The ATM cash does not add net value on day one. It protects existing value. The distinction matters enormously for how an investor should frame the decision to hold, add, or exit.

### On the Phase Transition

The entire RKLB investment thesis is a single question: **can you hold through the bridge?**

On one side of the bridge is the Phase 1 reality — a capital-consuming development program, serial equity dilution, and pre-profitability losses. On the other side is Phase 2 — $1.85B in government contracts self-funding production, $200–400M/year in operating cash flow, and an ATM that becomes permanently unnecessary.

The tank requalification is the gate to the bridge. The static fire is the gate to the launch. The launch is the gate to Phase 2.

**For a long-term investor:** The current price of $72 is effectively paying for the Electron business and the SDA backlog, and receiving Neutron almost for free. The $1.85B in confirmed backlog is the most concrete evidence that Phase 2 is not theoretical — it is contracted, partially pre-paid, and waiting. The only question is the engineering.
