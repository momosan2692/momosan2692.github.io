## 3. ATM Reference Guide — What Every Investor Must Know

### 3.1 What Is an ATM Offering?

An **at-the-market (ATM) equity offering** is a mechanism that allows a publicly listed company to sell newly issued shares directly into the open market, over time, at prevailing market prices — without a traditional underwritten deal, roadshow, or fixed discount.

Unlike a follow-on offering (where a bank underwrites a block at a fixed discount on a single day), an ATM is:

| Characteristic | ATM Offering | Traditional Follow-On |
|---|---|---|
| Timing | Continuous, over weeks/months | Single day or overnight |
| Price | Market price at time of sale | Fixed, typically 5–8% discount |
| Commitment | Discretionary — company can pause | Fully committed once priced |
| Transparency | Low (shares sold quietly through agents) | High (public roadshow) |
| Market impact | Gradual, sustained pressure | Sharp one-time drop |
| Dilution | Spread over time | Immediate and known |

**The key legal documents:**

| Document | Role | Timing |
|---|---|---|
| **S-3 Shelf Registration** | Pre-approval — reserves the right to sell up to $X | Filed months/years in advance |
| **424B5 Prospectus Supplement** | Activation — specifies exact amount, agents, use of proceeds | Filed the day the ATM launches |

---

### 3.2 How to Read a 424B5 Filing

The **424B5** is the single most important document an investor can read around an ATM event. It is filed with the SEC simultaneously with — or hours before — the public announcement. It contains the most legally binding disclosures the company will ever make, because any omission of material fact constitutes securities fraud.

**The two-document system in plain language:**

**Step 1 — S-3 Shelf (filed months/years in advance):**
> *"We reserve the right to sell up to $X billion in securities at some point in the future. Here is our general company information."*

General, forward-looking, no specific terms. The SEC reviews and approves it. It sits unused until needed.

**Step 2 — 424B5 Supplement (filed when the ATM activates):**
> *"We are now actually selling. Here is the exact amount, the exact agents, the exact mechanism, the exact use of proceeds, and the exact risk factors."*

The number code decoded:

| Code element | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **424** | The SEC rule family governing prospectus filings |
| **B** | Filed *after* the offering price is determined |
| **5** | A *supplement* to a previously filed base shelf prospectus |

**What to look for in a 424B5:**

1. **Aggregate offering amount** — the maximum the company can raise. This is a ceiling, not a commitment.
2. **Sales agents** — the banks executing the sales. More prestigious agents signal confidence.
3. **Use of proceeds** — how the money will be spent. Vague language ("general corporate purposes") is a yellow flag; specific language ("Neutron development, acquisitions") is more accountable.
4. **Prior program disclosure** — whether this ATM replaces an existing one and how much was drawn from it.
5. **Risk factors** — the most honest section. Management must disclose known material risks. Hardware failures, legal proceedings, and going-concern doubts all appear here first.
6. **Share count** — the current shares outstanding, which is the denominator for dilution math.
7. **Forward sale agreements** — advanced mechanism allowing the company to lock in a future price. Adds complexity but reduces price risk for the issuer.

**The mortgage analogy:**
- The **S-3 shelf** = getting pre-approved for a mortgage. The bank says "you qualify for up to $X." Nothing is borrowed yet.
- The **424B5** = actually drawing down on that pre-approval for a specific house at a specific price, with all terms documented and legally binding.

### 3.3 The Dilution Math — Correctly Calculated

The most common investor mistake around ATMs is using stale or wrong share counts. The dilution percentage is:

$$\text{Dilution} = \frac{\text{ATM Amount} \div \text{Stock Price}}{\text{Shares Outstanding}}$$

**Always use the most current shares outstanding from the filing itself — not from a financial data terminal, which may lag by a quarter.**

**RKLB example — the corrected math:**

| Input | Wrong (stale data) | Correct (from filing) |
|---|---|---|
| ATM size | $1,000,000,000 | $1,000,000,000 |
| Stock price | $72 | $72 |
| New shares needed | 13.9M | 13.9M |
| Shares outstanding used | ~408M (2024 figure) | **~605M** (Q1 2026 guided) |
| **Dilution** | **3.4%** | **2.3%** |

The 605M figure reflects convertible preferred share conversions and new shares from convertible notes in early Q1 2026 — none of which appear in older databases.

**Price sensitivity — same $1B ATM, different stock prices:**

| Stock Price | New Shares Needed | Dilution (605M base) |
|---|---|---|
| $80 | 12.5M | 2.1% |
| $72 (filing date) | 13.9M | 2.3% |
| $65 | 15.4M | 2.5% |
| $58 | 17.2M | 2.8% |
| $48 | 20.8M | 3.4% |
| $38 | 26.3M | 4.3% |

**The key insight:** A 10% stock drop on 2.3% dilution is a **4.3:1 overreaction on pure arithmetic**. The excess move reflects narrative risk and sentiment, not math.

### 3.4 The Reflexivity Loop — And Its Circuit Breaker

When an ATM hits, a potential feedback loop emerges:

```
ATM announced → stock drops → lower price → more shares needed to raise same amount
→ more dilution → stock drops further → more shares needed → ...
```

This is the **reflexivity loop**, and it is real — but it has a natural circuit breaker that most retail investors miss.

**The circuit breaker: management self-interest.**

The ATM is *not* a commitment to sell $1B immediately. It is maximum capacity sold at management's discretion. If the stock falls to $48, management can — and almost certainly would — **pause ATM selling**. Issuing 20.8M shares at $48 is structurally far more expensive for existing shareholders than issuing 12.5M shares at $80. Executives and the board own significant stock personally. They feel that dilution.

**Practical implication:** The reflexivity loop is bounded. The ATM creates sustained but time-limited downward pressure from a **price-agnostic seller** — the company, dripping shares through agents. Once the $1B is raised or management pauses, the mechanical pressure ends. This is fundamentally different from an informed seller with a negative view.

**RKLB historical pattern:**

| ATM Event | Immediate drop | Subsequent recovery |
|---|---|---|
| Sep 2025 ATM ($750M filed) | ~8% | +22% within 60 days (Neutron progress) |
| Jan 2026 tank failure | ~12% | Partial — recovered to ATH $99.58 |
| Mar 2026 ATM ($1B filed) | ~10% | **TBD — contingent on tank requalification** |

### 3.5 The Existing Shareholder's Edge

An investor who holds existing shares **and** has dry cash occupies a structurally advantaged position when an ATM drop occurs. Here is the logic:

**What the ATM seller actually is:**
- Does not have a fundamental view on the company
- Does not care if it sells at $72 or $68
- Will keep selling until the program is complete or management pauses
- Creates predictable, bounded, downward pressure

This is a **price-agnostic liquidity event** — not a panic seller, not an informed seller. When a non-fundamental seller meets a fundamental buyer at the same price, the fundamental buyer wins over time — as long as the underlying thesis is correct.

**What the existing shareholder can do:**

| Action | Effect |
|---|---|
| Hold existing position | Paper loss, same fundamental value — wait for pressure to end |
| Add new cash at lower price | Lowers blended cost basis, expands upside on total position |
| Average down aggressively | Amplifies both upside and downside — only justified with high conviction |
| Do nothing | Valid if position sizing was already appropriate |

**The honest risk assessment:**
The logic only works if the fundamental thesis is intact. If the reason the stock dropped is a real business problem — not just ATM mechanics — averaging down can compound losses. The investor must honestly ask: *is this drop about dilution mechanics, or is the business actually impaired?*

For RKLB in March 2026, the answer is: dilution mechanics + unresolved hardware risk. The distinction matters:
- **Dilution mechanics** alone → the drop is likely an overreaction → adding is defensible
- **Unresolved hardware risk** → the drop reflects genuine uncertainty → position sizing discipline is paramount

### 3.6 ATM Checklist Before You Act

When an ATM hits a stock you follow, work through this checklist before making any decision:

**Step 1 — Find and read the 424B5**
- [ ] What is the exact ATM size?
- [ ] Is this a new program or a replacement for a prior one? How much was drawn from the prior one?
- [ ] What is the stated use of proceeds?
- [ ] What risk factors are newly disclosed in this filing?
- [ ] What is the current shares outstanding per the filing?

**Step 2 — Calculate the correct dilution**
- [ ] New shares = ATM size ÷ current stock price
- [ ] Dilution % = new shares ÷ shares outstanding from filing
- [ ] Is the stock drop proportional to the actual dilution, or is it an overreaction?

**Step 3 — Assess the business context**
- [ ] Why is the company raising this capital? Is it opportunistic or necessity?
- [ ] Is there an unresolved fundamental problem disclosed in the filing?
- [ ] What does the cash runway look like with and without this raise?
- [ ] How many ATMs has this company done in the past 12 months? (Pattern matters)

**Step 4 — Evaluate your position**
- [ ] Is the drop about ATM mechanics, or a real business deterioration?
- [ ] What is your conviction level on the underlying thesis?
- [ ] Do you have dry powder to add, and is your position sizing appropriate?
- [ ] What is the catalyst that ends the ATM selling pressure?

**Step 5 — Decide and size**
- [ ] If adding: what is your maximum loss if the bear case materialises?
- [ ] If holding: is your current position size still appropriate given updated risks?
- [ ] If exiting: are you selling because of mechanics (likely wrong) or because the thesis is broken (possibly right)?
