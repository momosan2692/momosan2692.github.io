> **Abstract.** OpenAI's Jalapeño chip project — stalled at an $18B Microsoft financing impasse — is not primarily a deal-making failure. It is one data point in a structural convergence: every entity that owns a frontier AI model at scale will eventually own the silicon that serves it. This essay traces the IP boundary that separates meaningful vertical integration from a customer relationship with better branding, reconstructs the full game board beyond the OpenAI–Broadcom–Microsoft triangle, and reads Google's ten-generation TPU arc as the only completed proof-of-concept for where this all ends.

---

## Prologue: The Wrong Frame

When the news broke that Microsoft had declined to anchor the financing for OpenAI's custom chip program — effectively stalling Project Nexus before the first wafer was pulled from a TSMC furnace — most coverage framed it as a negotiation dispute. Two powerful companies couldn't agree on a deal structure. Move on.

That frame is wrong, or at least radically incomplete.

What's actually happening is a collision between a ten-year structural force and the short-term capital architecture of one company's infrastructure buildout. The structural force is this: **inference compute economics have crossed a threshold at which owning the silicon is no longer an option for frontier model owners — it is an inevitability**. The capital architecture problem is real and urgent. But it is the *symptom*, not the disease.

To understand the Jalapeño situation clearly, you need three things: a precise map of the IP ownership question, a complete view of who else is playing this game and how far along they are, and a working knowledge of the only fully-realized case study — Google's ten-year TPU program. This essay tries to provide all three.

## Part I: What Jalapeño Actually Is

### The Architecture

OpenAI's Project Nexus has two silicon programs running in parallel:

**Jalapeño** is the inference accelerator — the chip that will serve ChatGPT responses to users, run GPT model forward passes at production scale, and ultimately determine what OpenAI pays per query at the hardware layer. It is being designed in partnership with Broadcom, manufactured on TSMC's 3-nanometer process node, and was initially targeted for volume production in 2026.

**Titan** is the training accelerator — the chip for building future models, also on TSMC N3, targeting late 2026. A second-generation training chip, **Titan 2**, is already in planning on TSMC's forthcoming A16 process node, meaning OpenAI is attempting to overlap design generations in the way mature chip companies do.

The broader structural architecture is a **systolic array compute die** — the same mathematical approach Google pioneered in TPU v1 — paired with HBM3E or HBM4 memory, Broadcom's SerDes for high-speed interconnect, and Broadcom's proprietary 3.5D XDSiP advanced packaging to assemble the full silicon stack into a deployable accelerator card.

The internal team is approximately 40 engineers, led by **Richard Ho** — who ran Google's Cloud TPU program. His presence is the single clearest signal of where OpenAI is trying to go: not a first-generation experiment, but a deliberate attempt to compress Google's decade-long vertical integration arc into something faster.

### The Financing Impasse

Broadcom's condition for underwriting the first phase of Project Nexus: Microsoft must commit to purchasing roughly 40% of the Jalapeño chip output — approximately $18B worth — and install them in Azure data centers under a leaseback arrangement where OpenAI rents compute back.

Microsoft has not signed. The official explanation is a disagreement over data center specifications: OpenAI envisions purpose-built facilities optimized for its custom silicon, while Microsoft prefers its standard, versatile Azure data center designs that can serve multiple workload types.

The deeper explanation is structural. Over the past 18 months, the Microsoft–OpenAI relationship has undergone a quiet but fundamental transformation. Microsoft traded its exclusive access rights for perpetual, royalty-free IP access to OpenAI's models through 2032. Azure remains OpenAI's primary cloud provider — but is no longer its only one. The exclusivity that justified massive, OpenAI-specific infrastructure commitments no longer exists. Asking Microsoft to anchor a $18B hardware financing structure on behalf of a non-exclusive partner runs precisely counter to the direction MSFT has been steering this relationship.

There is also a competitive dimension that rarely gets mentioned: Microsoft has its own inference chip program. **Maia 200** — a Marvell-partnered TSMC 3nm design claiming 3× the FP4 performance of Amazon's Trainium3 — is deployed in Azure's data centers and powers GPT-5.2 and Microsoft Copilot workloads. Committing $18B to Jalapeño chips, which compete directly with Maia 200 for the same inference rack space, is not a natural thing for Microsoft's infrastructure team to recommend.

## Part II: The IP Boundary Question

This is the most important analytical question in the entire Jalapeño story, and the one that gets discussed least precisely: **at what layer of the silicon stack does OpenAI need to own the IP, and at what layer can it safely license or outsource?**

![Three IP Ownership Models](/assets/img/header/2026-05-11/fig1-three-ip-models.svg)

*Fig 1: Hard-IP assembly vs self-defined inference core vs spec-level delegation — layer-by-layer IP ownership and strategic assessment*

### The Three Models

There are three coherent positions, not a continuous spectrum:

**Model A — Hard-IP Assembly.** OpenAI uses pre-built, commercially licensed compute blocks (ARM cores, Synopsys or Cadence systolic array IP, generic memory controllers) and acts as a system integrator assembling existing IP into a custom form factor. Broadcom handles SoC integration and packaging. OpenAI owns no RTL at any layer.

This model produces a chip quickly and cheaply. It produces exactly zero competitive differentiation. A generic systolic array optimized for no particular workload is, by definition, not optimized for GPT-class inference patterns — the specific attention computation shapes, KV cache access patterns, MoE routing topologies, and speculative decoding flows that OpenAI's model teams have spent years learning. Model A is also commercially defenseless: Broadcom can sell the same integration to any OpenAI competitor without violating any IP agreement.

**Model B — Self-defined Inference Engine Core.** OpenAI's internal team designs and owns the RTL for the **XPU compute die**: the systolic array (matrix multiply unit), the attention kernel engine, the KV cache manager with LLM-inference-specific memory scheduling, the MoE routing fabric, and the ISA and compiler interface co-designed with OpenAI's model software team. Broadcom provides what it uniquely can: SerDes IP, HBM4 PHY, PCIe and Ethernet MAC, and the 3.5D XDSiP packaging that integrates the compute die with memory and I/O tiles into a deployable accelerator. Broadcom also handles physical implementation, design-for-test, and manufacturing coordination with TSMC.

This is the current path. Richard Ho's 40-person team is building Model B. The IP boundary is precise: OpenAI owns the compute die RTL and the compiler stack. Broadcom owns the interconnect, PHY, and packaging.

**Model C — Spec-Level Delegation.** OpenAI provides a performance specification — target TOPS, latency requirements, memory bandwidth targets, power envelope — and Broadcom designs the full chip to meet it, owning all micro-architecture and RTL. OpenAI is, in this model, a sophisticated customer writing a very expensive purchase order.

The strategic problem with Model C is not that it produces a bad chip. Broadcom is extremely good at ASIC design. The problem is that Broadcom, in this scenario, owns the IP that determines OpenAI's inference cost structure in perpetuity. When OpenAI's model changes — when a new attention mechanism requires different datapath widths, or a new MoE routing strategy requires different on-chip memory topology — the path to implementing that change runs through Broadcom's design team, on Broadcom's schedule, at Broadcom's price. **OpenAI becomes a Broadcom customer, not a Broadcom partner.** The distinction is not semantic. It is the difference between having leverage and not having it.

### The IP Boundary That Actually Matters

The boundary between Model B and Model C is not arbitrary. It corresponds to a precise engineering distinction in the silicon stack:

**The compute die** (systolic array + attention engine + KV cache manager) is where workload-specific optimization lives. The performance difference between a generic matrix multiply unit and one tiled specifically for GPT-class attention head counts, context lengths, and batch size distributions can be substantial — not in peak FLOP/s, which is a marketing number, but in actual inference throughput per watt at production serving conditions. This is also the layer where speculative decoding algorithms, prefix caching strategies, and continuous batching policies get embedded in hardware. OpenAI must own this layer's RTL.

**The PHY and SerDes** are the high-speed analog and mixed-signal layers that physically connect the compute die to HBM memory stacks and to other accelerators across a network fabric. This is one of the hardest disciplines in semiconductor engineering. Broadcom and Marvell have spent decades building SerDes IP libraries with characterized jitter performance, process corners, and layout rules across multiple TSMC nodes. No company with 40 engineers builds this from scratch on a three-year timeline. Google — with a decade of TPU experience, hundreds of chip engineers, and unlimited capital — still licenses Broadcom's SerDes for TPU training workloads. The PHY/SerDes layer is correctly outsourced.

**The packaging layer** (Broadcom's 3.5D XDSiP, TSMC's CoWoS) is where compute dies, HBM memory stacks, and I/O tiles are physically assembled using advanced heterogeneous integration. This is an equipment-intensive manufacturing competency, not a design competency. No chip company "owns" this layer in the sense of performing it in-house — they partner with packaging houses, and the packaging IP is the packaging house's property. This will remain true regardless of how large OpenAI's internal team grows.

> **The correct summary:** own the compute die RTL and compiler stack; license the SerDes and PHY; co-design the packaging integration with Broadcom. This is not a compromise position — it is the analytically correct allocation of where OpenAI can build differentiated IP versus where commoditized industry IP is the right answer.

## Part III: Self-Project Team vs Strategic ASIC Partner

The question of whether OpenAI needs an in-house chip team, a strategic ASIC partner, or both can be decomposed into six precise arguments — three on each side.

![Self-Project Team vs Strategic ASIC Partner](/assets/img/header/2026-05-11/fig4-self-vs-partner.svg)

*Fig 4: Three decisive reasons for each position, and the phased hybrid that resolves them*

### Why an In-House Team Is Non-Negotiable

**The model-silicon co-design loop cannot be outsourced.** OpenAI's inference chip needs to reflect what OpenAI's model team has learned about the actual compute patterns of frontier model serving: how KV cache access patterns change with context length, how MoE routing topologies affect memory bandwidth requirements, how speculative decoding trees interact with batch scheduling. This knowledge is OpenAI's crown jewel. It is not in any academic paper. It cannot be conveyed to Broadcom's design team in a specification document — the knowledge is tacit, iterative, and changes with every model generation. The only way to embed it in silicon is to have people who understand both the model and the hardware working in the same team.

**IP ownership is the only protection against partner rent extraction.** The Google TPU program's history is instructive here. After years of deep dependency, Broadcom raised its TPU design service fees by approximately 30%. Google's response — reportedly planning to bring TPU design fully in-house starting from 2027, and diversifying to MediaTek and Marvell for new chip programs — illustrates the endgame of Model C dependency. The irony is that OpenAI is attempting to break free from Nvidia's pricing power by building its own chip, while potentially recreating the same dynamic one level down the stack with Broadcom. The only protection is owning the RTL.

**Roadmap autonomy requires internal foresight.** A chip designed today must anticipate the model architecture of 18–24 months from now, because that is the lead time between design tape-out and volume deployment. OpenAI's Titan 2 is already being planned on TSMC's A16 process while Titan 1 is still in production ramp on N3 — this overlapping generation cadence is only possible because there is an internal team that understands the model roadmap and can begin the next chip specification before the current one is finished. Outsourcing chip design to Broadcom would mean Broadcom sets the cadence, which means OpenAI is perpetually serving yesterday's models on next year's silicon.

### Why a Strategic ASIC Partner Remains Structurally Necessary

**The partner's IP took decades to build and cannot be replicated quickly.** Broadcom's SerDes library for advanced nodes, its 3.5D XDSiP packaging platform, its HBM interface IP, and its physical implementation methodologies represent an accumulated engineering asset that took more than five years of focused investment to build — and that is the recent version, built on decades of prior SerDes and networking silicon work. A team of 40 engineers, however talented, cannot replicate this on the timeline required to tape out in 2026. More to the point, there is no good reason to replicate it. These are not differentiating layers for OpenAI.

**Full-stack chip design requires far more than 40 engineers.** The disciplines involved in a production AI accelerator are numerous and distinct: RTL design and verification, physical implementation (place-and-route), static timing analysis, design-for-test (DFT) and scan insertion, mixed-signal PHY design, package co-design, HBM interface characterization, thermal and power analysis, reliability qualification, and manufacturing coordination with TSMC's foundry process. A team of 40 can own the architectural specification and the compute die RTL. It cannot staff all of these competencies. Broadcom's value is not just its IP — it is also the hundreds of engineers executing the layers that OpenAI's team cannot realistically staff.

**Foundry and packaging dependence is structurally permanent.** TSMC manufactures approximately 92% of AI chips at advanced nodes (7nm and below). Every chip mentioned in this essay — Google TPU, Amazon Trainium, Microsoft Maia, Meta MTIA, and OpenAI's planned Jalapeño — is manufactured at TSMC. This is not changing. Similarly, advanced heterogeneous packaging (CoWoS, 3.5D XDSiP) requires the equipment, process recipes, and quality systems of a dedicated packaging operation. No AI company is vertically integrating into wafer fabrication or advanced packaging manufacturing. The partner layer is permanent at these levels; the question is only which partners and on what terms.

### The Synthesis

The correct answer is a phased hybrid with a hard IP boundary: the in-house team owns the compute die RTL and the compiler/ISA stack from the beginning, and the strategic partner executes PHY, SerDes, and packaging with licensed IP. The boundary evolves over time — as the internal team scales from 40 to 200 to eventually hundreds of engineers, some SerDes and PHY work can be internalized — but the packaging and foundry layers remain permanently outsourced.

This is not a theoretical position. It is, precisely, the model Google has been refining for ten years.

## Part IV: The Google TPU — Ten Years as the Only Proof

![Google TPU Vertical Integration Timeline](/assets/img/header/2026-05-11/fig2-google-tpu-timeline.svg)

*Fig 2: From v1 inference-only internal chip (2015) to v7 Ironwood commercial external deployment (2025)*

Google began designing its Tensor Processing Unit in 2013, shipped the first version internally in 2015, and publicly acknowledged its existence at Google I/O 2016 when AlphaGo used it to defeat Lee Sedol. Eleven years later, the seventh-generation TPU — Ironwood — is deployed at one-million-chip scale, available commercially through Google Cloud, and serves as the primary compute substrate for Anthropic's Claude inference workloads under a 3.5-gigawatt, multi-year contract starting in 2027.

That arc is worth studying carefully, because it is the only completed existence proof for the path OpenAI is attempting to compress.

### The Generation-by-Generation Logic

**TPU v1 (2015): Inference Only, Internal Only.** The first TPU was deliberately narrow. It handled matrix multiplications for neural network inference — specifically the type of computation needed to run AlphaGo's policy and value networks quickly enough to beat a human professional. It was not a training chip. It was not sold externally. It was a bet that Google's inference workload was large enough and predictable enough to justify the fixed cost of custom silicon. At this stage, Broadcom was already the design partner, providing exactly the services it provides today: physical implementation, SerDes, and packaging.

**TPU v2–v3 (2018–2020): Training Capability and Scale.** Version 2 added the distributed shared memory and high-bandwidth interconnect needed for training — a qualitatively different design challenge from inference. Version 3 introduced liquid cooling, enabling the higher power envelopes that large-scale training requires. The key architectural lesson: training and inference turn out to want different things from silicon, but Google spent two generations building a chip that tried to do both reasonably well before accepting that tradeoff.

**TPU v4 (2022): The Networking Investment.** Version 4 introduced the 3D torus interconnect topology — connecting each chip to six neighbors rather than four — which dramatically reduced the communication latency for the all-reduce operations that dominate large-scale training. A v4 pod contains 4,096 chips. The maximum hop count between any two chips in a 4,096-chip pod dropped from approximately 128 to 48. This generation also added the Sparse Core, a small specialized processor for embedding table lookups that delivers 5–7× acceleration for recommendation-system-adjacent workloads while consuming only about 5% of chip area.

**TPU v6 Trillium (2024): The LLM Native Generation.** Trillium was the first TPU generation explicitly designed around the Transformer architecture's specific compute patterns — adding an MLP core optimized for the feed-forward network computations that dominate LLM compute budgets. It delivered 4.7× peak performance over v5e while improving energy efficiency by 67%. Significantly, Trillium is manufactured on the same TSMC N5 node as v5p but achieves 2× the peak FLOP/s — the gain came from micro-architectural optimization, not process node advancement.

**TPU v7 Ironwood (2025): The Inference-First Declaration.** Ironwood is the first TPU explicitly designed for inference at massive scale. The architectural shift is significant: instead of the MegaCore architecture (a single logical core with unified memory) used in v4 and v5p, Ironwood adopts a chiplet design with two chiplets per package, each with its own 96GB HBM3e stack and connected via a die-to-die interface six times faster than inter-chip interconnect links. A single Ironwood Superpod integrates 9,216 chips, delivers 42.5 FP8 exaflops, and operates at 2× the performance-per-watt of Trillium.

The commercial milestone: Ironwood is the first TPU offered externally through Google Cloud at scale, and Anthropic has committed to using more than one million Ironwood chips for Claude inference — making it the first custom ASIC to reach seven-figure deployment volume at a single customer.

**TPU v8 (2026): The Split That Validates Everything.** The forthcoming eighth generation is the most analytically significant. For the first time, Google is splitting training and inference into separate chip designs — v8t for training, v8i for inference. Every prior generation was a single chip asked to handle both workloads, accepting compromises in each direction. The split is Google's acknowledgment, in silicon, that the two workloads have fundamentally different hardware requirements — and that the inference market is large enough to justify a dedicated chip. This is the structural validation of the inference ASIC thesis: if Google is convinced that inference deserves its own chip after a decade of building combined chips, the argument is settled.

Simultaneously, Google is reportedly in discussions with Marvell Technology to develop two additional chips — a memory processing unit and a new inference-optimized TPU — adding a third design partner alongside Broadcom and MediaTek. The multi-partner strategy is deliberate: each partner knows the others exist, creating competitive pressure that limits any single partner's pricing power. This is where the Google TPU program has arrived after a decade. It is where every mature custom ASIC program eventually arrives.

### What This Means for OpenAI

OpenAI is at year zero of a program Google has been running for eleven years. The relevant question is not whether OpenAI can replicate Google's outcome — it can, eventually — but whether it has the capital structure, organizational patience, and financing flexibility to survive the early generations, which are always more expensive and less performant than the incumbent GPU alternative.

Google could absorb three to four generations of TPU that underperformed Nvidia's contemporary GPU because Google's internal workload (Search, ads, Gmail) provided a captive demand base that justified the investment even before TPUs were competitive on a pure performance basis. OpenAI does not have that same captive base — it depends on external compute providers (currently Nvidia, Cerebras, and AWS) for the inference capacity it cannot yet self-supply.

The Jalapeño financing impasse is, at its root, OpenAI's attempt to solve the chicken-and-egg problem: it needs the chip to build the demand base that justifies the chip. Microsoft's leaseback commitment would have been the bridge financing that made that loop close. Without it, OpenAI has to find another bridge — a restructured financing arrangement, a different anchor customer, or a willingness to accept a slower initial deployment scale.

## Part V: The Full Game Board

The OpenAI–Broadcom–Microsoft triangle is a financing structure. The actual game board is much larger.

![Inference ASIC Full Game Board 2026](/assets/img/header/2026-05-11/fig3-inference-landscape.svg)

*Fig 3: Beyond the triangle — every major frontier model owner converging on inference silicon*

### The Hyperscalers

**Google** occupies a category by itself: the only entity that has achieved full vertical integration of model, silicon, and cloud platform. More than 75% of Gemini's computation runs on TPUs. Google Cloud sells TPU access externally, and Anthropic's multi-year, 3.5-gigawatt contract starting in 2027 demonstrates that Google's TPU program is now commercially viable as a standalone business — not just an internal cost-reduction exercise. Broadcom's AI revenue runs at approximately $8.4 billion annually, with Google as its primary anchor customer.

The multi-partner move — adding MediaTek for cost-optimized inference chips and now potentially Marvell for a memory processing unit — signals that Google has matured past the single-partner phase and is managing its chip supply chain the way a semiconductor company manages its foundry relationships: with redundancy, competition, and generational optionality.

**Amazon AWS** built its custom silicon program differently: without owning a frontier model, AWS needed an external anchor customer to justify the investment. Anthropic's $25 billion commitment to Trainium provided that. The Trainium3 chip — 2.52 PFLOPS of FP8 compute, 144GB of HBM3e, assembled into 144-chip "UltraServer" liquid-cooled racks — is by AWS's own benchmarks competitive with Nvidia's Blackwell architecture at the rack level, at significantly better power efficiency. The chip IP came through Annapurna Labs, which AWS acquired in 2015 for approximately $350 million — one of the most underappreciated acquisitions in semiconductor history.

**Microsoft's** Maia program is strategically distinct: it is explicitly inference-only, making no pretense of training capability. Maia 200, designed with Marvell and manufactured on TSMC 3nm with over 140 billion transistors, delivers more than 10 PFLOPS of FP4 compute. Microsoft's claim of 3× FP4 performance versus Trainium3 is aggressive and not independently verified, but even if half true, represents a meaningful efficiency gain for the Copilot and Azure OpenAI Services workloads it currently serves. The strategic insight is correct: for Microsoft's purposes, training happens at OpenAI's facilities (on OpenAI's compute), and Microsoft only needs to serve inference at Azure scale. Maia 200 is calibrated for that problem specifically.

**Meta** is perhaps the most analytically interesting case because it made the most aggressive architectural choice: RISC-V. The MTIA (Meta Training and Inference Accelerator) is built on a RISC-V ISA, which means Meta is permanently outside the ARM licensing ecosystem. The current v2 "Artemis" chip handles recommendation engine inference — the workload that has historically justified Meta's largest GPU clusters. The roadmap (MTIA 300–500 series, claiming 25× compute gains across four generations) and 2026 capital expenditure of approximately $135 billion — the majority directed at MTIA rather than Nvidia GPU purchases — represents the most decisive "replace Nvidia" bet of any company in this landscape. Meta is also the only player in this analysis that commits to open-source model distribution (Llama), which means it will never sell inference-as-a-service externally — it is building silicon purely to reduce its own unit economics.

### The Non-Hyperscaler Players

**Anthropic** is the clearest illustration that the convergence law has exceptions: entities that can afford to rent at hyperscale. Anthropic uses Google's TPU for inference (over one million Ironwood chips) and AWS Trainium for training ($25 billion commitment). This dual-provider strategy gives Anthropic access to cutting-edge silicon without the capital expenditure of building it, but at the cost of dual dependency — on Google for inference pricing and availability, and on Amazon for training capacity. If Google decides to monetize Ironwood differently, or if AWS's Trainium roadmap diverges from Anthropic's model architecture needs, Anthropic has no silicon alternative. For now, the scale and financial terms of its hyperscaler relationships make this an acceptable tradeoff. Whether it remains acceptable as Anthropic scales is an open question.

**Groq and Cerebras** represent a third model: pure-play inference specialists without model ownership. Groq's LPU (Language Processing Unit) uses deterministic static scheduling — pre-computing the execution schedule at compile time and eliminating the dynamic dispatch overhead that costs GPU cycles — to achieve exceptionally low inference latency: 276–300 tokens per second on Llama 3.3 70B, with speculative decoding pushing above 1,600 tokens per second. Cerebras uses wafer-scale integration (the entire wafer is a single chip, eliminating inter-chip communication entirely for models that fit) and has a $20 billion commitment from OpenAI for training compute. Both have the best inference silicon for specific workloads; neither can survive long-term without owning a model, because they are permanently dependent on their customers' architectural choices.

## Part VI: The Convergence Law

![The Inference ASIC Convergence Law](/assets/img/header/2026-05-11/fig5-convergence-law.svg)

*Fig 5: Three structural numbers that validate the convergence — and why the OpenAI financing impasse is a symptom, not a cause*

Three numbers define the structural context:

**70%**: The share of all AI compute accounted for by inference workloads in 2026. Training gets the press coverage — the $100 billion GPU clusters, the frontier model competitions — but inference is where the compute budget actually goes. A deployed model serves millions of users continuously. A training run happens once. This ratio is what makes inference silicon economics matter enough to justify multi-billion-dollar custom ASIC programs.

**44.6%**: The projected CAGR for custom AI ASIC shipments from cloud providers in 2026, versus 16.1% for GPU shipments (TrendForce). This divergence is the market's vote: inference is the growth workload, and inference is where ASICs beat GPUs on price-performance. The custom ASIC market is projected to reach $118 billion by 2033; Bloomberg Intelligence projects the total AI accelerator market at $604 billion by 2033. The share shift is structural, not cyclical.

**10 years**: Google's journey from TPU v1 (2015) to the first commercially deployed, externally available, inference-first chip in the seventh generation (2025). This is the realistic timeline for building vertically integrated silicon infrastructure from scratch — not because the engineering is slow, but because each generation requires the previous generation's lessons, and those lessons take time to accumulate.

The convergence law, stated precisely: **every entity that owns a frontier model at scale will eventually own the silicon that serves it, because at scale the inference cost structure is the margin structure, and owning the margin structure requires owning the hardware.**

Google proved the endpoint in ten years. Amazon proved that you can build the silicon without owning the model, if you have the right anchor customer (Anthropic). Microsoft proved that inference-only ASICs justify the investment even for a company with no model ownership. Meta proved that the most aggressive path — full architectural independence on RISC-V, $135 billion capex, zero external commercialization — is viable if your model distribution strategy is open-source. OpenAI's Jalapeño program is the newest entrant to this law's gravitational field.

The $18B financing impasse does not suspend the law. It delays OpenAI's entry into it. The question is whether the delay creates a competitive disadvantage that compounds — whether OpenAI's cost per inference query diverges so far from what Google pays per TPU query that model quality can no longer compensate. At current scales and inference volumes, the gap is manageable. At the scale OpenAI is planning — the 10-gigawatt data center buildout that Project Nexus anchors — it becomes existential.

## Epilogue: The Game Rule

The OpenAI–Broadcom–Microsoft triangle is not the game. It is a financing structure inside the game. The game is the convergence law — the structural force that is pulling every frontier model owner toward silicon ownership regardless of whether their bankers can agree on a leaseback arrangement.

The IP boundary question has a precise answer: own the compute die RTL and the compiler stack. License the SerDes and PHY. Co-design the packaging with Broadcom and eventually with multiple partners. This boundary is not arbitrary — it corresponds exactly to where Google drew the line after a decade of iterative learning, and it corresponds to where the differentiated value of inference optimization actually lives in the silicon stack.

The self-team-versus-partner debate has a precise answer: both, in a specific configuration. The in-house team owns the architectural specification, the compute die RTL, and the model-silicon co-design feedback loop. The strategic partner executes the layers that require IP which cannot be built on a three-year timeline. As the team scales from 40 to 200 to eventually hundreds of engineers, the boundary of what is internalized can shift — but the packaging and foundry layers remain permanently outsourced.

And the broader game has a precise endpoint: a landscape in which every entity serving frontier AI inference at commercial scale — whether Google, Amazon, Microsoft, Meta, or eventually OpenAI — operates its own custom silicon program, with TSMC as the permanent shared foundry and Broadcom, Marvell, and their successors as the permanent shared packaging and interconnect partners. Nvidia remains dominant in training and in the long tail of flexible compute. But inference, at scale, will increasingly run on silicon designed by the people who also designed the models running on it.

OpenAI is at year zero. The law is patient.
